The confusing world of dental care

In a world where we are constantly bombarded by facts, half-facts, and downright myths – like “non-fluoride toothpaste is better for your teeth” or “you must rinse with mouthwash to kill bacteria” – it is no wonder we feel lost.

Your overall health is reflected by the state of your mouth. Bleeding gums are a sign of inflammation, and frequent bad breath or sensitivity can hint at deeper issues. These signs should not be ignored.

Inspired by Nadine Artemis’ book Holistic Dental Care and current scientific studies, this confusing world of dental advice can be simplified into seven clear, effective steps – creating a holistic routine.

  1. Oil pulling
    Oil pulling is an Ayurvedic practice where you swish a small amount of oil (preferably cold-pressed organic coconut oil) in your mouth for around 5-15 minutes. A meta-analysis concluded that oil pulling significantly reduces total bacterial counts in the mouth. Including this step at the very start of your morning routine may help with bad breath, plaque levels, and mild gum inflammation.
  2. Salt rinse
    The enamel on your teeth is primarily made of hydroxyapatite, a crystalline calcium phosphate that’s susceptible to acid dissolution when your mouth’s pH drops below 5.5. A saltwater rinse is a mildly alkaline solution, helping to slightly raise saliva pH, reducing the risk of enamel softening – especially helpful if you brush right after eating.

    You can easily mix up a salt rinse at home:
    • 300 mL of natural spring water
    • 6 grams of salt
    • 1 drop of peppermint oil (for freshness)
    • 1 drop of frankincense oil (anti-inflammatory)
  3. Tongue scraping
    Another practice rooted in Ayurveda is tongue scraping, often done with a copper scraper, since copper has mild antimicrobial properties. Randomized trials have shown that tongue scraping significantly reduces volatile sulphur compounds – the primary cause of bad breath – and does so more effectively than brushing the tongue with a toothbrush.
  4. Brushing gums
    Carefully brushing the gums toward the teeth stimulates blood flow, which helps keep gum tissue resilient and supports healing – much like dry brushing the skin boosts circulation. Many holistic practitioners, including Nadine Artemis, emphasize gentle gum brushing. Mainstream dentists agree: brushing along the gum line is essential to disrupt plaque where it builds up first.
  5. Brushing teeth
    When it comes to plaque and gum health, powered toothbrushes consistently outperform manual brushing. Studies show they remove plaque and reduce gum inflammation, making them especially helpful for anyone prone to gingivitis. Unlike Nadine Artemis’ holistic preference for non-fluoride options, decades of high-quality research demonstrate that fluoride toothpaste remains the single most effective way to prevent cavities.
  6. Floss, pick, or whatever suits you
    Whether you prefer traditional floss, water flossers, or soft picks, what matters is cleaning between the teeth every day. This is where most cavities and gum issues begin.
  7. Salt rinse (again)
    Finishing your routine with a gentle salt rinse helps soothe the gums, balance your mouth’s pH, and wash away lingering acids. It’s a simple way to leave your mouth feeling calm and refreshed.

What to avoid?

  • Alcohol-based mouthwashes can help reduce bacteria in the short-term, but studies show there’s no meaningful difference vs. alcohol-free rinses. Alcohol-free is generally gentler, causing less irritation and dryness.
  • Common toothpaste additives are worth a closer look. Inspired by Nadine Artemis’ philosophy, many people choose to avoid ingredients like:
    • Sodium lauryl sulphate (SLS): linked to gum irritation and more frequent mouth ulcers.
    • Artificial colours, sweeteners, or preservatives: research suggests they could impact the oral or gut microbiome.
    • Triclosan: once common for plaque control, now largely phased out over safety concerns.

    • Bottom line

    In the end, your mouth is the gateway to your body – and caring for it does not have to be complicated. By combining holistic practices with solid science, you can create a dental care ritual that feels good, nurtures your mouth, and supports lifelong health.
