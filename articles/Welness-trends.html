WELLNESS TRENDS

The amount of wellness “hacks” trending right now is higher than ever. Scroll for five minutes and influencers in perfect lightning are swearing by a new device, powder, or ritual that changed their life. It is overwhelming. Where does genuine self-care end, and marketing begin?

Here, we explore some of the most talked-about wellness practices, what science actually says, and whether they are worth your time – and money.

RED LIGHT THERAPY

LED masks, full-body panels, and even handheld devices – you name it. Red light therapy (RLT) is being promoted as the answer to wrinkles, hair loss, fatigue, and even brain fog.

What research shows:

  • RLT (also known as photobiomodulation or low-level light therapy) works by stimulating mitochondria in skin cells, boosting cellular energy and repair. Early studies suggest improvement in skin texture, collagen production, acne lesions, wound healing, and pain reduction.

Winter vs. sunlight:

Red and near-infrared light are naturally present in sunlight. If you spend a lot of time outdoors, you already get exposure to these wavelengths. However, in winter months—especially in northern latitudes—sunlight is weaker, days are shorter, and people spend more time indoors. In those conditions, RLT can act as a supportive tool to mimic part of sunlight’s spectrum (the red/NIR portion) that promotes cellular repair and circulation. Source

VIBRATION PLATES

They are not new, but today’s wellness scene has reframed them as a shortcut to muscle tone, lymphatic flow, and fat loss.

What research shows:

  • Whole-body vibration (WBV) can improve muscle activation, bone density, and circulation.
  • Yet, WBV does not match the benefits of conventional movement like walking or resistance training. It is a supplement, not a substitute.

MOUTH TAPE

The TikTok hack everyone is whispering about: tape your mouth at night to force nose-breathing. Better sleep, energy, and even facial alignment, they say.

What research shows:

  • Chronic mouth breathing is tied to dry mouth, gum issues, suboptimal sleep, and even shifts in facial structure over time Source.
  • Nasal breathing produces nitric oxide, which helps dilate blood vessels, improve circulation, filter and humidify air, lower blood pressure, and may improve oxygen delivery, all contributing to better and deeper sleep. Source

Safety note: If you try mouth taping, use medical-grade tape and a small vertical strip to allow airflow. It is not recommended for people with sleep apnea or breathing difficulties without medical guidance. Source

ROSEMARY WATER (AND OIL)

A light spray of rosemary-infused water? It is the latest “natural tonics” trend touted for shiny, thicker hair.

What research shows

  • Clinical trials found rosemary oil to be as effective as 2% minoxidil for promoting hair growth after six months – with fewer side effects like scalp itching. Source
  • It likely works as a vasodilator and offers antioxidant and anti-inflammatory support to follicles. Source

Simple recipe:

  1. Add filtered water and a handful of organic rosemary leaves to a pan.
  2. Heat this for 15 minutes, be careful not to let the water boil (to preserve the nutrients from the rosemary).
  3. Let the mixture cool down before you add it into a spray bottle.
  4. Spray onto your scalp daily and gently massage in – no need to rinse.

ELECTROLYTES

Fancy powders promise hydration, energy, and recovery – but do we really need them?

What research shows:

  • Electrolytes (sodium, potassium, magnesium) are vital for hydration and muscle function.
  • For most healthy people, plain water and dietary sources cover electrolyte needs. Powders can be useful in endurance sports or extreme conditions—but are rarely necessary day-to-day.
  • Many powders are just upscale flavoured salt – with added sugar and marketing hype.

BOTTOM LINE

When we feel well on the inside, we radiate beauty on the outside. Trends can be fun to explore – but your body already has powerful, efficient cycles of healing and balance.

These practices can support rather than substitute those cycles. Remember, true wellness does not come from powders, panels, or quick fixes. Instead, it is built from nourishing habits – sleep, movement, proper hydration, whole foods, and routines that are gentle, sustainable, and self-affirming.

Wellness is listening, not chasing.
